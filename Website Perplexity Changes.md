I reviewed the live site structure and several live calculator pages, and the site has a solid foundation but needs a more systematic publishing and UX pass to compete in finance search. The strongest positives are the privacy-first positioning, simple language, and practical tool concept, while the main gaps are incomplete homepage elements, inconsistent page uniqueness, and thin trust/SEO scaffolding around the calculators and education pages.

Homepage action plan
The homepage already communicates a strong promise around “clarity and comfort” and on-device privacy, which is a real differentiator in personal finance. The first priority is to make the homepage feel fully finished and more navigationally powerful by repairing the incomplete FAQ entries, adding stronger paths into each calculator category, and linking each featured tool to at least one related article and glossary term.

I would make these changes first:

Replace the blank FAQ items with 6–8 complete user-facing questions and answers, because incomplete FAQ content weakens trust immediately.

Add a “Start here” section for new visitors, for example: budgeting, debt, mortgage, retirement, and net worth.

Add a short “How our calculators work” block with methodology, update frequency, and data-source language to reinforce credibility in a YMYL niche.

About and trust pages
Your About page does a good job explaining the mission, privacy stance, and accessibility of the site, but it still needs more visible expertise and editorial discipline to rank well in finance. In this niche, mission is helpful, but Google and users also want to know who is responsible for the content, what qualifies them, and how the material is reviewed.

I would add:

Founder/editor name and biography with relevant experience.

Editorial policy and review standards.

Calculator methodology standards, including data sources and update timing.

A visible “not individualized financial advice” line linked to the disclaimer page.

Calculator review
The live calculator pages I could verify are functionally promising and communicate clearly in plain English, which suits your audience well. The Debt Cost Calculator explains the output clearly and updates instantly, while the Food Inflation Calculator uses a practical framing tied to budget impact and salary burden, both of which are useful consumer angles.

The main improvements needed across all calculators are:

Unique page intros of 120–200 words explaining what the calculator does, who it is for, when to use it, and what the outputs mean, because several pages still look sparse in search and snippet form.

A methodology section under each calculator explaining formulas, assumptions, and data source timing.

Related links under each tool, for example “read this next,” “related calculators,” and “key terms”.

Structured outputs that can be summarized in an SEO-friendly paragraph under the live results area.

Calculator-wide standards
Every calculator page should follow the same template so the site feels authoritative and scalable in a competitive finance niche. That template should include:

One-paragraph intro.

Calculator.

Plain-English result explanation.

Formula and assumptions.

Data sources and update note.

3–5 FAQs.

Related article links.

Related calculator links.

Key terms from the glossary.

This matters because finance calculators alone are often too thin to rank strongly unless they are wrapped in explanatory content and trust signals.

Highest-return next steps
If you want the best sequence for impact, I would do it in this order:

Repair the homepage FAQ and strengthen homepage internal linking.

Standardize the calculator template across every tool.

Rewrite titles, meta descriptions, and intros for the calculators and weaker education pages.

Add author, review, methodology, and source-trust elements site-wide.

Build topic clusters so calculators, glossary, and education pages reinforce each other structurally.

A realistic strategic view is that TopMoneyTools is already directionally right, but it still looks more like a good project than a fully optimized finance publisher. The next stage is not reinvention; it is disciplined finishing, stronger trust architecture, and deeper calculator-page completeness.
